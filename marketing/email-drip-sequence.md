## Email 2 — Day 2

**Subject:** What your Big Five results actually mean

**Preview text:** Hint: it's not about being an introvert.

---

Hi {{firstName}},

The Big Five is the most rigorously studied personality model in psychology. But most people only get the surface version: "You're 72% extroverted." End of story.

That number means much more than it appears.

The Big Five measures five dimensions: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism (sometimes called Emotional Stability). Each one describes a spectrum — not a box you're in, but a tendency you lean toward under normal conditions.

Here's what makes it genuinely useful:

**Your traits describe patterns, not limits.** High conscientiousness doesn't mean you're rigid. High neuroticism doesn't mean you're broken. Each trait exists because it conferred advantages in certain environments. Understanding yours helps you work with your tendencies instead of fighting them.

**Traits are contextual.** You might be extroverted at work and introverted at home. That's not contradiction — that's nuance. The Big Five captures your baseline, not every version of you.

**The real insight is in the combinations.** High openness + high conscientiousness often produces extraordinary creative discipline. High agreeableness + high neuroticism can make someone exceptionally empathetic but prone to taking on others' stress. Your profile is the intersection of all five.

## Email 3 — Day 5

**Subject:** The psychology of your relationships

**Preview text:** Your attachment style shapes more than you think.

---

Hi {{firstName}},

Every relationship you've ever had — romantic, professional, familial — has been shaped by something you probably can't see clearly: your attachment style.

Attachment theory, developed by John Bowlby and expanded by decades of research, describes the emotional strategies we learned in childhood to stay connected to the people we needed. Those strategies become so automatic that we carry them into adulthood without realizing it.

There are four main styles:

**Secure** — comfortable with closeness, able to ask for what you need, resilient when relationships get hard.

**Anxious** — deeply attuned to others, but prone to worrying about abandonment or misreading silence as rejection.

**Avoidant** — values independence, tends to pull away when things get emotionally intense, often reads neediness in others that may not be there.

**Disorganized (Fearful-Avoidant)** — wants closeness but fears it, often showing up in push-pull relationship patterns.

Knowing your attachment style doesn't excuse behavior — it explains the root. That's where change becomes possible.

Paired with your Enneagram type (which describes your core motivational pattern), you get a map of *why* you show up the way you do in relationships — and what a healthier version looks like.

Hi {{firstName}},

Carl Jung spent decades studying the patterns that appear across every culture's mythology, literature, and dreams. He found something strange: the same characters kept appearing. The Hero. The Shadow. The Sage. The Trickster.

He called them archetypes — primordial patterns woven into the human psyche.

Here's why this matters to you:

Most of us are shaped by 2-3 dominant archetypes. They influence what kinds of work feel meaningful, how we instinctively respond to conflict, and what we're reaching for when we set goals.

Take the **Caregiver** archetype. People with a strong Caregiver pattern feel deeply compelled to nurture, support, and protect. At their best, they create communities and heal broken things. Under stress, they can burn out giving more than they receive — because helping *feels like survival*.

Or the **Explorer**. They're driven by freedom, novelty, and the horizon. Put them in a stable, predictable environment and they feel like they're disappearing. Give them open terrain and they'll map things nobody thought to look for.

Neither archetype is better. But each one suggests something real about what kind of life feels like *yours* rather than a life you've talked yourself into.
