## Day 5 — Data/Research

**Topic: Answer + Attachment Style and team performance**

Yesterday's poll: Which Big Five trait predicts leadership most?

The research answer: **Emotional Stability** (low Neuroticism).

More than charisma. More than intelligence. More than work ethic.

Leaders who stay calm under pressure create psychologically safe environments. And psychological safety — as Google's Project Aristotle found — is the #1 predictor of high-performing teams.

But here's what the Big Five misses: **attachment patterns**.

Attachment theory (originally developed for parent-child bonds) turns out to apply powerfully to workplace relationships:

→ Secure attachment → trusts team, delegates well, seeks feedback
→ Anxious attachment → micromanages, needs constant validation, struggles with criticism
→ Avoidant attachment → conflict-averse, emotionally distant, resists vulnerability

Research shows leaders with insecure attachment styles underperform on team retention, conflict resolution, and employee satisfaction — regardless of their trait profile.

Personality is multidimensional. Leaders who understand their attachment patterns outperform those who don't.

## Day 8 — Thought Leadership

**Topic: Values alignment is the hidden driver of career satisfaction**

Gallup says 85% of employees are disengaged at work.

Here's what they don't tell you: disengagement is almost always a values mismatch.

Schwartz Values Theory (validated across 80+ countries) identifies 10 universal human values:
- Self-Direction, Stimulation, Hedonism, Achievement, Power
- Security, Conformity, Tradition, Benevolence, Universalism

People are most motivated when their daily work activates their top 2-3 values.

A high-Achievement, high-Power person in a flat, consensus-driven organization will feel constantly frustrated — even if the role is "perfect on paper."

A high-Benevolence, high-Universalism person in a hyper-competitive sales culture will feel morally drained — even if they're hitting numbers.

The mismatch isn't a character flaw. It's an alignment problem.

Most organizations do skills matching. Almost none do values matching.

The ones that do — have dramatically lower turnover.
